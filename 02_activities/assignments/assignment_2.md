    Good Visualization: Climate Spiral by Ed Hawkins
The "Climate Spiral" visualization by climate scientist Ed Hawkins effectively conveys global temperature anomalies from 1850 onwards. This animated visualization has gained significant recognition, including being featured in the 2016 Summer Olympics opening ceremony. The Climate Spiral exemplifies good data visualization due to its clarity, intuitive structure, and impactful communication of climate change trends.
Clarity and Intuitiveness
A fundamental principle of effective data visualization is that it should allow viewers to understand complex data easily (Few, 2012). The Climate Spiral achieves this by leveraging the cyclical nature of time, where each loop represents a year, and each segment denotes a month. The animation format enables users to track temperature trends over time seamlessly. Additionally, the visualization employs clear axis labels and distinct color gradients to enhance comprehension, aligning with Tufte’s (2001) guidelines on data clarity.
Visual Impact and Engagement
The Climate Spiral uses a gradient of blue to red hues, effectively illustrating temperature changes. The outward expansion of the spiral visually emphasizes global warming, making the data compelling and memorable. 
Dissemination and Influence
The widespread adoption and sharing of the Climate Spiral indicate its success as an impactful visualization (McCandless, 2010). The animation format fosters a more profound emotional response, reinforcing climate change awareness. Unlike static charts, the movement aspect aligns with human cognitive processing patterns, enhancing message retention.
The Climate Spiral is an exemplary data visualization because it effectively combines clarity, accuracy, and engagement. 
Bad Visualization: Misleading Line Chart in Housing Article
The Australian Broadcasting Corporation (ABC) published a line chart in an article about housing availability, intending to illustrate how unoccupied homes could contribute to resolving the housing crisis. However, the visualization fails to communicate the data effectively due to poor design choices, including improper chart selection, misleading representation, and lack of clarity.
Inappropriate Use of Line Chart
In this visualization, the data categories are discrete (e.g., types of unoccupied housing stock), making a bar chart a more appropriate choice. Using a line chart falsely implies a continuous relationship between these categories, leading to potential misinterpretation by viewers.
Misleading Representation of 'Total'
One of the most critical errors in the visualization is the inclusion of 'Total' as a data point within the same series as individual categories. This violates the principle of categorical clarity (Tufte, 2001), as it creates a misleading impression of how the subcategories relate to the whole. Misrepresenting totals within a series of parts can distort the viewer’s understanding of the dataset’s hierarchy.
Lack of Clear Labeling
The ABC chart lacks adequate labels and context, making it difficult for viewers to decipher what each line represents. Without clear explanations, data visualizations can lead to confusion rather than enlightenment (Cairo, 2013).
This housing crisis line chart exemplifies poor data visualization due to its incorrect use of a line chart, misleading inclusion of totals, and lack of clear labeling. Such visualizations can distort the message and misinform audiences, highlighting the importance of adhering to best practices in data representation.

The housing line chart suffers from misleading design choices that could be improved by selecting a more appropriate chart type, such as a bar chart, to represent discrete categories instead of implying a continuous trend.
